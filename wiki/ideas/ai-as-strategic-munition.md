# AI as a Strategic Munition

## The Insight
The governance of frontier AI has shifted from "safety guardrails" (software-based restrictions) to "export controls" (state-level law). High-reasoning capabilities are no longer viewed as a commercial service provided by a lab, but as a strategic munition. When a model's capability crosses a threshold where it can be used for large-scale cyber warfare or intelligence gathering, the state asserts direct control over the "weights" and API access, treating intelligence itself as a controlled substance.

## Evidence
- [[entities/claude-mythos]] — The US government's order to shut down global access to Mythos 5 demonstrates a treatment of the model as a strategic asset rather than a commercial product.
- [[entities/claude-fable-5]] — The global shutdown of Fable 5 via a "digital kill switch" based on export control directives.
- [[sources/us_government]] — The Commerce Department's use of export control directives to force Anthropic to suspend access for foreign nationals.
- **G7 Summit (June 17, 2026)** — Tensions peaked when US export blocks on Mythos 5 and Fable 5 led G7 leaders to express concern over US control of AI, sparking calls for a "trusted partners" scheme to ensure digital sovereignty.

## Implications
This marks the end of the "Global Brain" illusion. AI progress will no longer be a unified global ascent, but a fragmented competition where capabilities are siloed into national blocs. This inevitably leads to a "Cognitive Cold War," where the most capable models are guarded like nuclear secrets, and access is granted only based on geopolitical alignment. The "Cold War" has shifted from the hardware (chips) to the weights themselves.
