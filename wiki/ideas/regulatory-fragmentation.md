# Regulatory Fragmentation

## The Insight
The EU's AI Act rollback — a 16-month delay plus industrial exemptions for manufacturing AI — represents the first major reversal of AI safety regulation globally. Combined with the NIST binding agreements signed by Google, Microsoft, and xAI (while Anthropic, Meta, and OpenAI notably abstained), the world has fragmented into three irreconcilable regulatory blocs: the US pursuing military-first deployment with CAISI pre-testing, the EU choosing competitiveness over precaution, and China accelerating state-directed development with no private-sector restrictions. For the first time, AI companies face meaningfully different rules in different markets — and the winners will be those with legal teams large enough to navigate the fragmentation, not necessarily those with the best models.

## Implications
The three-tier global landscape creates a compliance nightmare. Labs now need teams larger than their safety research departments just to track diverging rules: EU industrial exemptions, US defense partnerships, Chinese efficiency mandates, and emerging state-level regulations. The fragmentation favors incumbents with legal resources — Anthropic and OpenAI may have better models, but Google and Microsoft have better government relations.

The EU's 16-month delay creates a window where European AI development is less constrained but also less certain. German manufacturing (Siemens, Bosch) won the exemption they threatened to move R&D for, but the political calculus — choosing competitiveness over precaution — may backfire if a high-profile AI incident occurs during the delay period.

Colorado's approach — disclosure over prohibition — gives businesses flexibility while protecting consumers. But it also creates a patchwork: businesses operating nationally must comply with Colorado's requirements even if other states have no equivalent. The notification requirement mirrors GDPR's transparency principles but adds enforcement teeth with an August 2026 deadline. Copycat legislation in blue states and preemption battles if federal standards emerge are likely.

The UK government's Claude chatbot deployment adds a third European regulatory thread: while the EU delays and debates, the UK is deploying AI in critical government services. This divergence within the Western bloc is as significant as the US-EU-China split — and creates competitive advantages for labs (like Anthropic) that can meet sovereign safety requirements.
