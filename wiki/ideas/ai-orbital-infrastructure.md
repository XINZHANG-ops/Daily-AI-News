# AI Orbital Infrastructure

## The Insight
NASA's Prithvi deployment marks the moment AI models became orbital infrastructure, not just software. By processing satellite imagery in space before transmitting to ground, NASA solves the bandwidth bottleneck that has constrained Earth observation for decades. The deeper implication: if models can run on radiation-hardened, power-constrained space-grade hardware, terrestrial edge deployment becomes trivial by comparison. And orbital AI opens a new geopolitical battleground — "AI in orbit" could become as strategically significant as GPS or satellite communications.

## Evidence
- [[entities/prithvi]] — First geospatial AI foundation model deployed in space; trained on 13 years of Landsat + Sentinel-2 data; dual deployment on Kanyini satellite and ISS
- [[sources/spacex]] — SpaceX's Starshield military constellation is the obvious commercial platform for orbital AI; Prithvi proves the technical feasibility
- [[topics/llm_models]] — Prithvi's 13-year training dataset represents a data moat approach distinct from frontier labs' scale-driven paradigm; NASA wins through exclusive data rather than parameter count
- [[ideas/local-ai-computing]] — Prithvi's on-orbit processing is an extreme form of edge computing; if AI works in space, terrestrial edge (nanobot, MOSS-TTS 0.1B) becomes technically trivial
- [[sources/google]] — Google's Gemini automotive rollout (~4M GM vehicles) and NASA's Prithvi represent opposite ends of the edge-deployment spectrum: one in cars, one in orbit

## Implications
The next frontier for AI is not bigger models but broader deployment. Terrestrial AI is saturating — every phone, car, and server already has a model running. Space-based edge computing opens genuinely new capabilities: real-time disaster response without bandwidth constraints, autonomous satellite operations, and eventually "AI constellations" that could rival GPS in strategic importance.

For the commercial sector, Prithvi validates that orbital AI is technically feasible now, not in a decade. SpaceX's Starshield and similar military constellations will almost certainly carry similar models. The question is whether orbital AI remains a government/national-security domain or becomes commercially accessible — and who controls the orbital compute infrastructure.
