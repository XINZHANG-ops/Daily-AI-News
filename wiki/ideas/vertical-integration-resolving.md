# Vertical Integration Resolving

## The Insight
The "wrapper vs foundation" debate in AI is resolving toward full-stack vertical integration. On May 12-13, 2026, three independent announcements confirmed the pattern: Krea launched Krea 2 (its first proprietary foundation image model built from scratch), Google announced Googlebook (laptops built around Gemini integration), and OpenAI launched Daybreak (a bundled cybersecurity platform with multiple GPT-5.5 variants). Survivors are building full stacks, not relying on public APIs.

## Evidence
- [[entities/krea-2]] — Krea's first proprietary foundation image model signals the "wrapper" phase is ending for creative AI; fine-grained style control is the differentiator generic models can't match
- [[entities/googlebook]] — Google betting the laptop category on Gemini integration; the OS battle shifting from app ecosystems to AI integration depth
- [[entities/openai-daybreak]] — Bundled cybersecurity platform with three GPT-5.5 variants; partners (CrowdStrike, Palo Alto) act as distribution channels
- [[sources/openai]] — OpenAI's pivot from Sora and Instant Checkout to enterprise superapp and Daybreak reflects investor pressure for sustainable unit economics through owned stacks
- [[sources/google]] — Google's $190B capex and full-stack ownership (TPU → Gemini → Cloud → Distribution) is the vertical-integration thesis at hyperscaler scale

## Implications
The pattern follows what happened with video: first everyone uses public APIs, then the survivors build their own models. Krea's survival bet is that creative professionals care more about controllable aesthetics than raw generation quality. Google's bet is that Gemini integration is the hook ChromeOS never had. OpenAI's bet is that bundling security models with distribution partners creates a moat that API access alone cannot.

For startups, this means the window for "wrapper" businesses is closing. The capital required to build proprietary models is enormous, but the differentiation it creates may be the only sustainable moat. For enterprises, it means evaluating AI vendors on their stack depth, not just their model benchmarks — a vendor that owns the full pipeline from training to deployment can iterate faster and control costs better than one that relies on third-party APIs.
