# Government Pre-Testing

## The Insight
The Trump administration's shift from hands-off deregulation to structured pre-release evaluation is the most consequential AI policy development of 2026. CAISI (Center for AI Standards and Innovation) has already completed 40+ evaluations, with some frontier models blocked from release. The critical design choice is "voluntary with teeth": Google, Microsoft, and xAI joined because the alternative was likely an executive order making it mandatory. Anthropic's Mythos model was the catalyst — when a company voluntarily refuses to release a model for safety reasons, it forces regulators to act. The parallel with FDA drug trials is intentional: CAISI is positioning itself as the approval gate for AI "therapeutics" that could harm national security.

## Evidence
- [[entities/caisi]] — 40+ evaluations completed with some frontier models blocked; ~200 staff trying to keep pace with 500+ model versions frontier labs will ship this year
- [[sources/google]] — Google volunteered for CAISI pre-testing alongside Microsoft and xAI; the alternative was reportedly a mandatory executive order the White House is drafting
- [[sources/microsoft]] — Microsoft joined CAISI while simultaneously deploying AI on Pentagon classified networks — the same company participates in safety evaluation and military deployment
- [[sources/xai]] — xAI joined CAISI despite Musk's anti-regulation rhetoric; "voluntary with teeth" means even the most deregulation-friendly labs recognize that unregulated deployment invites heavier intervention
- [[sources/anthropic]] — Anthropic's Mythos model was the catalyst for CAISI; when Anthropic voluntarily refused public release, it created regulatory momentum that now affects all frontier labs
- [[sources/openai]] — OpenAI renegotiated Biden-era agreements to align with Trump's AI Action Plan; participation is defensive — better to shape the framework than be shaped by it
- [[topics/ai_safety]] — EU AI Act Phase 2 fines on Mistral (€11.2M) and Stability AI (€8.4M) prove Europe is taking a harder line than the US, creating a balkanized regulatory landscape where models need different disclosures and evaluations for each jurisdiction
- [[ideas/institutional-gap]] — CAISI's 200 staff vs 500+ model versions per year is the institutional gap made quantitative: governance cannot keep pace with deployment velocity

## Implications
The compliance cost alone will favor incumbents who can afford legal teams over startups. A balkanized regulatory landscape is emerging where models need different training-data disclosures, red-team documentation, and risk evaluations for each jurisdiction. The "voluntary with teeth" design may become the global template: governments offer legitimacy, labs offer compliance, and the public gets neither transparency nor meaningful accountability. The real question is whether pre-testing can scale faster than model development — and whether 200 staff at CAISI can evaluate 500+ model versions per year without becoming a rubber stamp.
