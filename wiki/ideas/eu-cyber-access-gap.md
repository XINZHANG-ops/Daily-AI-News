# EU Cyber Access Gap

## The Insight
OpenAI's "Trusted Access for Cyber" program granting EU institutions access to GPT-5.5-Cyber, contrasted with Anthropic restricting Mythos to ~12 US tech firms and denying EU regulators preview access, creates a structural disadvantage for EU financial institutions. Germany's Bundesbank publicly demanded Mythos access, warning that European banks cannot test defenses against AI-powered threats they cannot evaluate. This divergence comes just as Google's Threat Intelligence Group confirmed AI-developed zero-days in the wild, making Mythos's vulnerability-hunting capabilities more urgent than theoretical.

## Evidence
- [[sources/openai]] — George Osborne (former UK Chancellor) positioned OpenAI's EU cyber access as a geopolitical win: "Europe's many defenders, not just the few"
- [[sources/anthropic]] — Mythos restricted to ~12 US tech firms; EU regulators denied preview access despite 4-5 meetings; Germany's Bundesbank publicly demanded access
- [[sources/google]] — Google's Threat Intelligence Group identified and blocked the first confirmed AI-developed zero-day exploit; "high confidence" it was AI-developed
- [[entities/claude-mythos]] — Mythos's vulnerability-hunting capabilities are exactly what EU banks need to test defenses against AI-powered threats
- [[entities/gpt-5.4-cyber]] — OpenAI's EU cyber program gives European institutions defensive capabilities Anthropic refuses to provide

## Implications
The EU access gap is not a regulatory annoyance — it is an active security liability. Attackers already have AI-powered tools (the Google-confirmed zero-day was AI-developed), while defenders in Europe are structurally blind without equivalent capabilities. The geopolitical dimension is explicit: OpenAI's Osborne framing positions cyber model access as a alliance-building tool, while Anthropic's delay risks alienating a major economic bloc.

This creates a three-way tension: OpenAI builds geopolitical relationships through access, Anthropic maintains safety through restriction, and EU institutions become dependent on whichever US lab chooses to share. The dependency itself is a strategic vulnerability.
